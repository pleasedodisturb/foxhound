## How I Actually Work

I'm energetic, people-driven, and genuinely chaotic — in the best way I know how to describe it. My brain works like a browser with 40 tabs open. I know a lot about a lot of things, and I'm not deep in any single one. That used to feel like a weakness. It isn't. It's the whole point of me.

What I love is *connecting things*. Taking a service here, an integration there, a Slack feature nobody noticed, a Jira hook that no one bothered to set up — and making them talk to each other. It's not work for me, it's exploration. It's the same feeling I used to get browsing Product Hunt at midnight, finding some weird little tool and going down a rabbit hole. The AI bubble has made that scene exhausting and subscription-heavy, but the instinct is still there. I like this kind of chaos.

The best illustration: I didn't know anything about vibe coding when I joined [Previous Company]. Then I started building with a colleague, understood how it worked, and now I'm building AI pipelines for fun. That's how I learn. I need to touch the thing. I need to break it and rebuild it. I don't front-load theory — I start, fail fast, adjust, keep going.

I came to [Previous Company] to be a one-man army. Build stuff. Adapt. Overcome. Never be a function. That's still exactly what I want to be.

## What "Growth" Means to Me (and What It Doesn't)

I don't want to grow into a Staff Program Manager who owns a spreadsheet empire and has a carefully maintained LinkedIn presence full of "stakeholder alignment." That's not growth to me. That's calcification with a pay raise.

The growth I'm after is harder to define and much harder to find. It's growing into more complex problems. Building things I couldn't have built six months ago. Learning by doing, not by adding a certification. Getting feedback that stings — and being better for it.

I also have a clear line on what growth *above a median threshold* tends to require in big orgs: making decisions that aren't right, and hiding behind your title or HR to avoid accountability. I've watched people do it. I won't do it. I'd rather stay closer to the ground where honesty is still possible.

I want a modern, next-level thing. I don't want to hide behind frameworks.

## My Philosophy on Process

Here's what I actually believe: no framework will save you. Not SAFe, not OKRs, not sprint ceremonies, not a beautifully maintained Confluence. These things are useful tools, not religions.

My approach is something like an umbrella method: take everything we know — frameworks, AI, collective knowledge, everything we've learned from watching things fail — and apply what works *in this context, right now*. Test it. If it doesn't work, kill it. Iterate — not just in two-week sprints, but daily. Hourly. Constant small reassessment.

The more open and flexible the system, the easier this becomes. Rigidity is where good ideas go to die.

## The Last Role Lesson

I was a change agent in an environment that had already given up on change. Everybody complained about the chaos. Nobody wanted anything that would reduce it. The implicit rule was: *just sit down and chill, it's a mess anyway.*

I kept showing up. Posting links, sharing ideas, writing in channels, getting excited about things. Getting ignored. At some point, you stop writing. Not because you've given up on the ideas — but because the silence becomes louder than any negative feedback. Silence is its own message.

What I didn't get was critically painful feedback. That was new. No one told me I was wrong. They just... didn't engage. And for someone like me, who needs a live system to work with, an environment that doesn't respond is the hardest kind.

I understand I'm part of the problem — I'm chaotic, I move fast, I push. I'm genuinely open to feedback. I'll self-evaluate out loud. But that has to go somewhere. You can't debug a system that won't send errors.

## The Environment That Brings Out My Best

One that *wants* to change. Not performatively — actually wants it.

One where connecting things across domains is valued, not treated as scope creep. Where someone who built an AI-augmented workflow gets a "tell me more" instead of a "that's not in the roadmap." Where experimentation is baked in, not scheduled. Where chaos is a feature, not a failure mode.

I flourish in 0-to-1. Give me something undefined and hard and I'll find a way through it. Give me something fixed and defended and I'll slow down and eventually stop.

Also: give me real feedback. I need it. I want it. I don't break.

## The Environment That Kills Me

Ignore-and-endure culture. The place where you post a good idea and nobody reacts — not even a thumbs down. Just silence.

Framework worship — where the process is more important than the outcome, where nobody remembers why the process exists.

"Spreadsheet seniority" — where you earn authority by producing documentation, not by building things or solving real problems.

And on values: I will not work for companies whose primary business model is data extraction and surveillance. That's not an aesthetic preference. Google Ads, Meta, Amazon Ads — it's not about size. It's about what they are and what they do. Being inside one of those machines would cause genuine moral and mental distress. That's a hard line.
