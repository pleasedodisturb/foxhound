# The Wolt Story: Lessons Learned & Career Positioning

## What Happened

Joined Wolt (DoorDash) in Frankfurt as a Technical Program Manager in October 2024. Fired during probation in February 2025 after 4 months.

**Comp:** 125k EUR base + 40k DoorDash RSUs

**The assignment:** Managing a complex, politically charged Salesforce implementation alongside a product manager and program manager for the business side.

**The stated reason for termination:** "Strong reluctance to work within existing people, processes, and tools. Tendency to introduce new tools and processes tailored to own vision, disruptive to teammates."

## What Actually Happened

The "Engineering Operations" team (TPMs at Wolt) presented as tech-forward and collaborative during hiring. The reality was different:

- **Innovation theater:** Team members publicly championed tech-forwardness but operated like it was 2010 -- manual Google Sheets, static Confluence pages for milestones and timelines, all-day meeting cycles for status reporting.
- **Staff TPMs as status-quo keepers:** Experienced team members had accumulated deep organizational knowledge but hadn't built any shared methodology. "There is no Wolt way -- you just figure it out."
- **Political minefield disguised as friendly culture:** The team appeared collaborative but was actually a group of compliant, salary-attached operators under leadership (Head of TPMs / now Managing Director of Wolt Germany) who valued conformity over impact.
- **AI as a threat, not a tool:** Built a comprehensive AI-powered TPM system in Cursor (Claude Code + MCPs + API integrations) that could automate significant chunks of TPM menial work. Team was publicly "impressed" but privately uninterested -- nobody asked questions, nobody wanted to adopt it.
- **Manager as executor:** The termination was delivered by the manager in an 8-minute ambush meeting scheduled 10 minutes in advance, reading from a script, offering no specifics beyond the one vague example about "disruption."

## What I Built There (and What It Demonstrates)

Despite the short tenure, built a functioning AI-powered TPM intelligence system:

- **Cursor environment** with Claude Code, OpenAI, Gemini, Glean integrations
- **Atlassian API** integration for ticket/project data
- **GSuite API** for document access
- **Slack via Glean MCP** for communication mining
- **Memory-bank and contextstream MCPs** for persistent agent context
- **Automated system sweeps** with one-liner prompts
- **Meeting transcript archival** with searchable context
- **Private gitignored folder** for off-the-record relationship/political notes that informed agent reasoning

This system could replace a significant portion of traditional TPM work: status gathering, timeline tracking, stakeholder mapping, risk identification, document synthesis.

## Honest Self-Assessment

Things I could have done differently:
- **Read the room earlier.** The signals were there -- the lack of curiosity about AI tools, the absence of any shared methodology, the careful politeness. These are markers of a team optimized for stability, not change.
- **Built allies before building systems.** Introduced tools before establishing trust and political capital. Should have spent the first 2-3 months doing things "their way" to earn credibility.
- **Managed up more carefully.** Didn't fully understand the Head of TPMs' priorities and incentives. In retrospect, my "disruptive" approach was a direct threat to the status quo that leadership was invested in maintaining.
- **Low conscientiousness is real.** The EPP score of 32nd percentile in conscientiousness isn't wrong -- I am naturally spontaneous and less structured. In an environment that valued visible process compliance, this was a liability.

## How to Position This

**The reframe:** "I was hired to bring technical program management to a complex Salesforce implementation. I quickly identified that the existing tooling and processes were inadequate for the scale of the challenge, and built an AI-augmented system that could dramatically improve TPM productivity. The organization wasn't ready for that level of change, and there was a cultural mismatch between my approach and the team's operating model. I learned important lessons about change management and organizational readiness that make me more effective today."

**Key narrative beats for interviews:**
1. Joined with genuine excitement, strong mandate language during hiring
2. Assessed the situation quickly (Strategic theme)
3. Built something genuinely innovative (Activator + Ideation)
4. Didn't navigate the politics well enough (lesson learned)
5. Left with integrity intact -- the farewell letter proves character under pressure
6. Applied the lessons immediately (this entire repo is evidence of that)

## What This Means for the Next Role

**Must-haves:**
- Genuine (not performative) openness to AI and automation
- Leadership that values outcomes over process compliance
- A problem complex enough to warrant creative solutions
- Autonomy to choose tools and approaches
- A team that's actually building things, not just reporting on them

**Red flags to watch for:**
- "We're very innovative" with no evidence
- All-Staff-TPM teams with no shared methodology
- Heavy meeting culture with no async alternatives
- Leadership that talks about AI but uses Google Sheets for everything
- Probation periods used as no-accountability firing windows
